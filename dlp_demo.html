Discrete Logarithm Problem

The Definition

The Discrete Logarithm Problem (DLP) is the foundation of Diffie-Hellman, RSA, and Elliptic Curve cryptography. It relies on a simple mathematical asymmetry: some operations are easy to perform but nearly impossible to reverse.

gx ≡ y (mod p)

Given g, x, and p, finding y is easy.
Given g, y, and p, finding x is extremely hard.

The "Easy" Direction

Modular exponentiation is the forward operation. Even with large numbers, we can use Binary Exponentiation to calculate the result in logarithmic time.

Example with small numbers: g = 5, p = 23, x = 6

56 mod 23 = 15,625 mod 23 = 8

Why it's Secure

As the prime number p grows, the number of possibilities becomes astronomical.

Key Size (bits) Number of Possibilities Classical Difficulty
8-bit ~256 Instant
512-bit ~10154 Hours (on PC)
2048-bit ~10616 Millions of Years

For a 2048-bit prime, there are more possibilities than atoms in the observable universe. This "computational gap" is what protects your data.
